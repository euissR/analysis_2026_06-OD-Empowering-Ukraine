Arming the Shield

How the EU can empower Ukraine against Russian aggression

by EUISS Research Team(1)

Russia's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 marked the most serious rupture of European security since the Second World War. More than three years later, the war continues to grind forward, with hundreds of thousands of casualties, millions of displaced persons, and Ukraine's economy reduced to a fraction of its pre-war scale. Yet Ukraine has not fallen. Against expectations, it has held — and in some domains, pushed back. The question this paper asks is not whether Ukraine can survive, but whether it can prevail, and what the European Union must do to make that possible.

The EU's response to the invasion has been unprecedented in scope. Since February 2022, Member States have collectively provided Ukraine with over €100 billion in financial, military and humanitarian assistance. Eleven packages of sanctions have targeted Russian energy, finance and defence. The European Peace Facility has broken long-standing taboos on EU arms transfers. Yet the pace and coherence of European support have fallen short of what the moment demands. A strategic rethink is overdue.

A closer look at Russia's strategic economic base — the resource wealth that funds the war machine — illustrates why. Moscow's Arctic territories generate the hydrocarbon revenues that finance the invasion. Yet Western sanctions have left significant loopholes: Rosatom's nuclear and logistics operations remain largely unsanctioned, Arctic LNG flows continue to European terminals via third-country transshipment, and a shadow fleet of ageing tankers ships Russian oil above the G7 price cap. Each of these gaps is a policy choice — not an inevitability.

The  Northern Sea Route (NSR)  is Moscow's instrument for monetising Arctic resources and circumventing Western sanctions. Russia's ability to route energy exports via the NSR limits the impact of EU pressure — a vulnerability that EU Arctic and sanctions policy must address directly.

Russia operates a network of port terminals along the NSR, managed by Rosatom — an entity that has so far escaped comprehensive EU and G7 sanctions. Closing this loophole is among the most concrete steps the EU could take to reduce Russian revenues and, with them, the Kremlin's capacity to wage war.

The data above illustrates a core paradox of EU strategy: the Union imposes sanctions on Russia while leaving significant revenue streams untouched. Shadow-fleet tankers carry Russian oil above the G7 price cap. Arctic LNG arrives in European terminals through third-country transshipment. Rosatom continues to operate freely across Europe's nuclear fuel supply chain. Closing these gaps requires not just political will but institutional capacity the EU has not yet fully developed.

EU support for Ukraine has evolved significantly since 2022, but three structural challenges continue to limit its effectiveness. Understanding these is essential to designing a more coherent approach.

From emergency response to strategic endurance: EU assistance has been reactive rather than strategic. Member States have donated what they could spare rather than what Ukraine most needs. The result is a patchwork of contributions — valuable in aggregate but suboptimal in composition. Ammunition shortages persist. Air-defence systems remain insufficient. Artillery barrels wear out faster than they are replaced. The EU has the economic weight to resolve these imbalances; it has lacked the coordination mechanisms and political will to do so consistently.(2)

The defence industrial base: Europe's defence industry was not built for a prolonged high-intensity land war. Production lines had been scaled back after the Cold War, and reconversion takes time. The EU has taken important steps — the Defence Production Support Instrument, the Act in Support of Ammunition Production — but ambition has repeatedly outrun delivery. Ukraine needs sustained industrial commitment stretching well beyond the current political cycle. The EU must treat its defence industrial ramp-up not as a temporary emergency measure but as a permanent structural investment.(3)

Institutional coherence and the unanimity constraint: EU foreign and security policy continues to operate under unanimity. This creates structural vulnerabilities: a single Member State can block, delay or dilute collective decisions. As the war has dragged on, divergences within the EU have grown. Some Member States advocate negotiated compromise; others insist on Ukrainian victory as the only basis for a durable peace. Managing this diversity while maintaining strategic coherence is the EU's central institutional challenge for the years ahead.(4)

These three challenges are not insurmountable — they are political choices. The question is whether the EU's Member States and its institutions can make the right ones before the window of strategic opportunity closes. Ukraine's resilience is remarkable, but it is not infinite. Europe's credibility as a security provider, its standing in the international order, and the safety of its own citizens all depend on getting this right.

In 2022, many European capitals hoped that the war would be resolved quickly and that the EU's role would be primarily humanitarian. That assumption has been overtaken by events. The EU now faces a choice between strategic commitment and strategic irrelevance. The analysis that follows shows what the former would require — and how far the Union remains from achieving it.

The pillars of EU support for Ukraine

This paper examines how EU support can be made more effective across the key instruments and domains where European action has the greatest potential to alter the strategic balance. We ask: what works, what falls short, and what bold steps could change the trajectory of the war? We address these questions in seven thematic chapters and five regional perspectives.

The instruments: what works and where the gaps are

The effectiveness of EU support varies considerably across different policy domains. In some areas, the EU has been able to act decisively, establishing new mechanisms and bypassing traditional constraints. In others, structural limitations — unanimity rules, industrial bottlenecks, member-state divergence — have blunted the impact of otherwise well-designed policies. The chapters that follow assess each instrument on its own terms, and ask what institutional or political changes would maximise its contribution to Ukraine's strategic position.

Energy security and sanctions enforcement: the EU has imposed eleven rounds of sanctions on Russian energy, but significant loopholes remain. This chapter examines how the EU can close the shadow-fleet gap, bring Rosatom under full sanctions coverage, and use its import market power to accelerate the erosion of Russian hydrocarbon revenues.

Military technology and defence industrial cooperation: Ukraine has become the world's most intensive testing ground for modern warfare. This chapter assesses how EU-Ukraine defence industrial partnerships can be deepened — joint production of artillery shells, drones and electronic-warfare systems — and what institutional frameworks would unlock the scale of cooperation required.

Financial support and macro-economic resilience: maintaining Ukraine's state capacity — paying salaries, pensions and energy subsidies — has been as important as military aid in keeping the country functional under fire. This chapter tracks the effectiveness of EU financial support mechanisms, including the Ukraine Facility, and examines whether frozen Russian sovereign assets can be sustainably mobilised for reconstruction.

Intelligence, surveillance and cyber defence: information asymmetry has shaped every phase of the conflict. This chapter evaluates EU contributions to Ukrainian intelligence capacity — satellite imagery, signals intelligence sharing and EU Cyber Rapid Response Teams — and identifies the legal and operational constraints that limit deeper cooperation.

Counter-disinformation and information resilience: Russia's information war targets not only Ukrainian society but European publics. This chapter examines how the EU can strengthen Ukraine's strategic communication capacity, protect European audiences from Kremlin narratives, and use regulatory tools — including the Digital Services Act — to reduce the reach of Russian disinformation.

Accession as a strategic instrument: EU membership has been described as the most powerful tool of European foreign policy. This chapter asks whether the accession process can be accelerated to serve as a strategic anchor for Ukraine — locking in reforms, sustaining elite commitment to the European project, and building the institutional infrastructure for post-war integration.

The frontline states: partners, lessons and vulnerabilities

Ukraine is not fighting alone, and the EU is not the only actor whose decisions shape the outcome. Countries on NATO's eastern flank — Poland, the Baltic states and Romania — have been Ukraine's most consistent advocates within the EU and its most important logistical partners. They have borne disproportionate security costs while pressing the Union to act with greater urgency. Beyond Europe's borders, the war has reshaped relationships across the globe — from the Gulf states recalibrating their energy dependence on Russia, to African governments navigating competing pressure from Moscow and Brussels.

The Baltic states and Poland have led EU ambition on Ukraine support, consistently advocating for higher military aid, tougher sanctions and faster accession. This chapter examines their strategic calculus — driven by acute threat perception — and asks what lessons their experience of Russian pressure and national resilience holds for the EU as a whole.

Romania and Moldova sit at the intersection of the war's most acute spillover effects — refugee flows, energy disruptions, Transnistrian instability. This chapter traces the security and economic costs borne by these two states, and makes the case for substantially deeper EU investment in their resilience and integration.

The South Caucasus has become a critical corridor for Russian sanctions evasion. Armenia, Georgia and Azerbaijan each navigate the war's pressures differently. This chapter assesses EU leverage in the region and the conditions under which a more active EU policy could reduce Russia's ability to circumvent Western pressure.

Countries across the Western Balkans face a difficult choice between EU accession commitments and economic ties with Russia. Several have declined to align with EU sanctions. This chapter examines how the EU can use enlargement conditionality and economic incentives to bring the Western Balkans into closer alignment with EU positions on the war.

The Global South has largely declined to condemn Russia. Many governments cite energy price shocks, food insecurity and historical non-alignment as reasons for abstention. This chapter analyses EU efforts to change this calculus — through the Global Gateway and grain-export diplomacy — and asks what more ambitious EU engagement in the Global South might look like.

Towards a comprehensive EU strategy
for Ukrainian victory

Europe faces a defining choice. The EU cannot be a serious geopolitical actor if it allows a revisionist power to redraw borders in Europe by force. Yet the path from rhetorical commitment to strategic effectiveness is neither obvious nor guaranteed. Member States disagree on how much risk to assume, how much to spend, and how far to escalate economic pressure on Russia. The EU's institutional machinery was not designed for sustained warfare.

The EU and its Member States must therefore develop a dual approach. In the Conclusion, the EUISS research team distils the lessons from this study into concrete recommendations. These span the short term — closing sanctions loopholes, accelerating ammunition production, deepening intelligence cooperation — and the structural: reforming EU decision-making on foreign and security policy, accelerating Ukrainian accession, and building the industrial and financial architecture for a long war and ultimate recovery. The mix between immediate and structural moves will vary across policy domains. Both are necessary to give Ukraine — and Europe — the best possible chance of a durable and just peace.
