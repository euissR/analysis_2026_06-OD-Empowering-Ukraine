The Middle East remains one of the most contested arenas for foreign-power competition. Decades of regional instability, vast energy resources, and the region's position at the crossroads of global trade routes have made it a magnet for external actors seeking to expand their influence, secure economic interests, and project military power far from their own borders.

The conflicts in Gaza and Sudan, the slow fragmentation of state authority across the Levant and Yemen, and the realignment of Gulf diplomacy have all created new openings for foreign engagement — and new risks of escalation. The analyses below map who is doing what, where, and with what tools.

Africa has emerged as a primary theatre for great-power competition in the twenty-first century. A combination of demographic growth, abundant natural resources, shifting governance structures, and frustration with Western conditionality has created fertile ground for a new generation of foreign actors — from established powers to ambitious middle powers — to expand their footprint across the continent.

The withdrawal or retrenchment of Western influence in parts of the Sahel, the Horn of Africa, and Central Africa has accelerated this dynamic, opening space for Russia's Wagner-successor operations, Chinese infrastructure diplomacy, Turkish soft-power networks, and Gulf investment.
