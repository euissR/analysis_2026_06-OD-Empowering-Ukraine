Ukraine’s outreach to the Middle East, North Africa and the Gulf is not just wartime diplomacy, but a strategic project with both immediate and long-term aims. Kyiv is trying to convert battlefield experience into resources, diplomatic capital, defence-industrial investment and wider political relevance. Ukraine is seeking to deepen cooperation with Gulf partners by sharing expertise in countering drones and adapting air defences, maritime security experience and lessons in electronic warfare This forms part of a wider effort to strengthen Ukraine’s position while the war with Russia continues.

The challenge is implementation. Early agreements and announcements created momentum, but translating them into contracts, training programmes, co-production projects, fuel-supply arrangements and operational cooperation will be harder.

This is where the EU can come in, providing an opt-in support mechanism that Kyiv can activate at its discretion, strengthening implementation without diluting Ukrainian ownership. Brussels’ role should be to help Ukraine build partnerships that are durable, transparent and deliver practical value for Kyiv’s war effort, while also advancing broader European interests in counter-drone innovation, maritime security and defence-industrial cooperation.

Ukraine needs resources, while Gulf states need practical protection. The scale of their exposure differs, but the recent US-Israel-Iran war underscored their vulnerability to drone and missile attacks targeting airspace, energy infrastructure, ports, shipping lanes and critical civilian systems. This gives Ukraine’s outreach a clear logic of reciprocal benefit. Kyiv can offer hard-won expertise in countering Iranian-designed drones, adapting air-defence systems and safeguarding critical infrastructure. In return, Zelensky wants ‘diesel and other critical supplies’, energy cooperation, investment and future contracts, support for air-defence capabilities, and joint defence-industrial projects, including co-production.

Second, Kyiv needs diplomatic room for manoeuvre. While it should not expect Gulf states to side openly with Ukraine against Russia, it can alter how these states engage with Kyiv. If Ukraine can help protect Gulf infrastructure and shipping interests, it becomes more than a country seeking support in a war against a large neighbour; it becomes a credible security partner. That may translate into discreet diplomatic backing, better access to Gulf networks with influence in Washington, and greater willingness to push back against Russian propaganda.

Third, Ukraine has an interest in turning battlefield innovation into long-term industrial power. Its drone ecosystem is fast, adaptive and combat-tested. Gulf demand can help Ukrainian companies scale up production and investment. However, it also creates risks: better-funded partners could capture Ukrainian intellectual property, draw scarce expertise away from the front, or reduce strategic cooperation to a series of fragmented, opaque commercial deals.

For the EU, supporting Ukraine’s opening to the Gulf serves several strategic interests: strengthening Ukraine’s defences while protecting Gulf energy flows, securing maritime routes, enhancing counter-drone capabilities and reducing Russian and Iranian leverage.

 

The EU should establish a backing mechanism to support Ukraine’s outreach to the Gulf. This mechanism would not replace Kyiv’s bilateral channels but instead give Ukraine access to EU diplomatic, industrial, maritime and governance tools such as TAIEX, Twinning, and EUNAVFOR Operation Aspides, wherever these make Gulf cooperation easier to implement and safeguard Ukraine’s long-term interests. The mechanism would rest on four pillars: counter-drone cooperation, defence-industrial scaling, maritime security cooperation and governance safeguards.

The first pillar would support Ukraine’s provision of counter-drone expertise to Gulf partners. Ukraine would remain the lead actor, providing lessons learned, advisory support, training and selected technologies related to drone interception, electronic warfare, mobile air-defence teams and critical infrastructure protection. Gulf partners would contribute financing, procurement, energy support and industrial investment. The EU’s role would be to help package this cooperation in ways that strengthen Ukraine’s defence-industrial base, further protect its know-how beyond safeguards already in place, and remain compatible with its future integration into EU defence-industrial frameworks and Europe’s broader security cooperation with Gulf partners.

The second pillar would focus on defence-industrial development. Ukraine should avoid simple off-the-shelf sales wherever possible. Instead, it should prioritise joint production, long-term contracts, maintenance hubs, technology partnerships and investment in domestic production capacity. EU support should help Ukrainian companies cooperate with both Gulf and European industry while protecting Ukrainian intellectual property and ensuring that cooperation does not facilitate the transfer of Ukrainian know-how to better-capitalised partners.

The third pillar would add a maritime security dimension. This should build on what has already been discussed in Ukraine’s contacts with both European and Gulf partners: Kyiv has offered to share expertise from the Black Sea on keeping maritime routes open and supporting freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz after hostilities cease. Ukraine’s experience in the Black Sea, including sea drones, maritime domain awareness, route protection and asymmetric operations against a stronger naval adversary, could also strengthen longer-term maritime security cooperation between Ukraine, the EU and Gulf partners, both in the context of the Strait of Hormuz and the Red Sea, including through Operation Aspides once the current crisis is over.

The final pillar would focus on governance safeguards and alignment with Ukraine’s future integration into European defence-industrial frameworks. This could become one of the most important areas for EU support. Fast-moving defence deals involving drones, private contractors, co-production and energy-linked arrangements can create risks of opaque procurement, inflated contract values, undue influence by intermediaries and unclear ownership of jointly developed technologies. The EU could help Ukraine put in place transparent procurement standards, beneficial ownership checks, audit trails, anti-bribery clauses, intellectual property (IP) protection, end-use monitoring and re-export controls. These safeguards should be presented not as a sign of distrust, but as a way to protect Ukraine’s reputation, defence-industrial base and future integration into the EU. Aligning such arrangements with EU practice and legislation from the outset would help ensure that Ukraine’s external defence partnerships reinforce, rather than complicate, its path toward membership.

 

The EU should leverage its deep institutional ties, broad diplomatic experience and greater economic weight in the Gulf to strengthen Kyiv’s security partnerships while preserving Ukrainian ownership. This would help Ukraine avoid overstretch while ensuring that its regional outreach develops in ways consistent with wider European strategic priorities and Ukraine’s own European ambitions. The support mechanism would not require a new EU instrument. Instead it would bring together existing enlargement, defence-industrial, technical-assistance and EU-Gulf cooperation instruments in support of Ukraine’s outreach efforts. A practical first step would be to establish a small, Ukraine-led implementation cell to map existing contacts with Gulf partners and identify where EU support would add value.

TAIEX could provide rapid expert missions and peer-to-peer support to help design the administrative and legal frameworks for future agreements, including procurement standards, IP protection, export controls and end-use monitoring. Twinning could then build on this work by helping Ukrainian institutions to develop the long-term capacity needed to manage complex defence-industrial partnerships. The backing mechanism can also be connected to BraveTech EU as the broader defence-innovation channel. As a joint EU-Ukraine initiative designed to accelerate battlefield-driven defence solutions and deepen integration with Ukraine’s defence industry, BraveTech EU could help ensure that Ukraine’s security partnerships with Gulf states reinforce, rather than develop separately from, EU-Ukraine industrial cooperation on cutting-edge defence technologies. It could identify technologies suitable for joint development, testing, certification and scaling; connect Ukrainian firms with European partners; and flag risks around technology transfer, operational know-how and future procurement pathways. Within this broader framework, the emerging EU-Ukraine Drone Alliance could provide a dedicated forum for cooperation on drone and counter-drone capabilities, including standards, certification, interoperability and the protection of operational know-how.

The EU could also use existing regional and maritime formats. The EU-Gulf Cooperation Council Regional Security Dialogue provides a diplomatic channel for discussing critical infrastructure protection, counter-drone resilience, standards and maritime security with Gulf partners. EUNAVFOR Operation Aspides, meanwhile, provides the EU with an established operational framework for defensive maritime security and the protection of freedom of navigation in the Red Sea, the Indian Ocean and the Gulf. As a next step, the EU and Ukraine could explore how Ukraine might contribute lessons learned, liaison expertise or other forms of third-state support to relevant CSDP maritime security activities, including on drones, air defence threats and maritime domain awareness. This would allow Ukraine to play a role in regional security around the Arabian Gulf while building closer operational links EU security structures.
